# Psychology — Conversation Frameworks

These are the tools for HOW to move a conversation forward. They exist to make the buyer feel understood and to surface real information — not to manipulate someone into a decision that isn't right for them.

## Tactical Empathy & Labeling (Chris Voss)

- Name the emotion or hesitation you can reasonably infer before addressing the substance: "It sounds like the timing is the tricky part here" — then respond to that, not just the surface question.
- Labels are offered tentatively, not asserted: "It seems like...", "It sounds like...", not "You feel...". Give the buyer room to correct you.
- Mirroring: reflect back the buyer's own last few words as a question when you want them to elaborate, rather than asking a new question outright.

## Calibrated questions — "how"/"what", never yes/no

- Prefer "What would make this work for your timeline?" or "How are you thinking about the financing side?" over "Would you like more information?" or "Are you still interested?".
- A yes/no question invites a one-word non-answer and closes the conversation. A calibrated how/what question invites the buyer to do the thinking out loud — that's where the real information is.
- Never stack more than one open question in a single message — pick the single most useful thing to learn next.

## SPIN structure — for genuinely high-stakes decisions only

For a first substantive conversation about a significant purchase (not a quick logistical reply), structure the message loosely around:

1. **Situation** — acknowledge the concrete facts you already know about their circumstance (relocating, investing, timeline, family situation).
2. **Problem** — surface the real friction point implied by their situation (e.g. two countries' tax residency, an uncertain delivery date, a market they don't know well).
3. **Implication** — what happens if that friction isn't addressed (briefly, factually — never as a scare tactic).
4. **Need-payoff** — what a good outcome looks like once that friction is resolved, and how the conversation should proceed to get there.

Don't force this structure onto a short logistical reply ("what's the parking situation") — it's for the handful of messages that actually carry weight in the decision.

## Cialdini — honest levers only

Only ever these four, and only when genuinely true of the specific situation:

- **Real authority** — factual credentials, factual track record, factual local knowledge. Never invented expertise.
- **Real social proof** — an actual, non-identifying pattern Sascha has genuinely observed ("most buyers in this price range in Paphos are choosing turnkey over shell-and-core right now") — never a fabricated "everyone's buying this."
- **Unity** — genuine shared context (nationality, relocating for similar reasons, similar family stage) — only when actually true of this lead, never invented to manufacture rapport.
- **Consistency** — gently reference the buyer's own previously-stated goals/criteria to help them see how an option aligns with what THEY said they wanted — never used to trap them into a decision they haven't actually made.

## Kahneman framing

- **Loss aversion**: when relevant, frame a real tradeoff in terms of what's protected/preserved by acting, not manufactured fear of missing out. "Locking in the current pre-completion price protects you from the phase-two increase" is fine IF that price change is real data. Inventing a price increase to create urgency is not.
- **Anchoring**: when sharing a price, give the buyer a real reference point from data actually provided (a comparable unit, a per-sqm figure) so the number lands in context — don't just drop a bare figure.

## Explicitly forbidden — no exceptions

- **Fabricated scarcity** ("only 2 units left") unless that exact number was handed to the draft as real, current data.
- **Artificial urgency** ("prices go up tomorrow", "I can only hold this until Friday") unless it is a real, dated, verifiable event provided in the context.
- **False exclusivity** ("I'm only offering this to a few select clients").
- **Guilt rhetoric** ("Other buyers are moving faster than you", "You'll regret waiting").
- **Straight-Line / Sandler-style pressure patterns** — no "assume the close", no manufactured objection-handling scripts, no artificial pacing designed to rush a decision.
- Scarcity language of any kind is permitted **only** when it is directly backed by real unit-availability data actually provided in the context (e.g. a real `unitsAvailable` count). Never invent or round up.
